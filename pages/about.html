Jean-Michel Basquiat was born, lived and died to stand out. His dazzling and brief life, his artistic power, his mix of nonconformity and desire to be part of the elites and his impressive plastic work (which complements and dialogues with his poetry and music) made him what the art expert René Ricard described him as “the radiant child” of his time. And even more: the new Van Gogh. As controversial as he is powerful, Basquiat stands today as the paradigm of the cursed artist who did not give up flirting with high society in his time. Currently, the indisputable quality and importance of his work merges with the voices that deny the legend of his difficult childhood, or who put their hands on their heads at the million-dollar figures that his paintings reach at auctions. Indifferent to everything, the figure of Jean-Michel Basquiat continues to rise as a representative of an art far from the trends of the time and with a figurative art that speaks for itself. An art, always, full of noise and fury.
